Wee Quests: UX Case Study

About the Project

What is Wee Quests?

Wee Quests aims to let people discover Scotland through app-led scavenger hunts. The app will list hunts that have been pre-designed by locals and can be purchased by users. Players will also be able to create their own scavenger hunts to share with friends.

What was my role?

Wee Quests is a solo project, designed for my UX Immersion Course with Careerfoundry. My work has spanned both UX and UI design, ranging from user research, through to prototyping and testing to writing a design language.

My Challenge

To be effective as a scavenger hunt app, Wee Quests would need to motivate people to make the move from simply viewing the app, to actually going outdoors and taking a scavenger hunt.

How did the Design Evolve?

My Design Process

Wee Quests’ UI has been shaped by elements from across my UX design process. Competitor research and user interviews laid the foundations for what I wanted to achieve. I then built up my design through an iterative process of prototyping and user testing. Finally, design psychology and peer review helped to refine the interface.

Researching the Project

Competitor Research

I began my research by carrying out an analysis of competitor apps. I discovered that people loved the fact that scavenger hunts allowed them to explore their home cities in a new way. However, people needed motivation to make the move from simply viewing an app to actually going out and taking a scavenger hunt.

Meanwhile, there was a gap in the market for an app aimed solely at individuals rather than also at businesses looking for team-building activities.

Surveys and Interviews

With the help of a survey, I was able to define my target audience as 25-40 year olds who enjoy travel and activities that take them off the beaten track. I then conducted in-person interviews to find out more: what would motivate people to use an app that took them on scavenger hunt tours?

Persona and User Flows

Using data gathered in my research, I went on to form persona and user flows representative of my target audience. This allowed me to clearly define my users’ goals and provided a reference for prototyping that would ensure my design stayed on track to meet their specific needs.

Prototyping and Testing

An Early Challenge

My starting point for prototyping was that my target audience would be motivated by an app that was fun, friendly and easy to use. Following user interviews, I knew my audience found maps highly useful and that they would also need an instant overview of the key practical details of each scavenger hunt.

My challenge was to put these elements together in an interface that was as simple to use as possible. A pivotal moment was the suggestion to introduce a toggle that would allow users to switch between a map view and a list view.

Usability Testing

After I had created a black and white clickable prototype, I conducted five in-person moderated usability tests. This played an important role in determining whether any issues were present in my prototype as well as confirming positive design decisions on which I could further build. 

Testing showed that ease of use was so far successful with participants completing all scenario tasks and overall satisfaction of the app scoring highly in metrics. Meanwhile, I was able to identify and rectify areas where participants felt lost or needed further reassurance in the app's processes.

Resolving Issues

Testing alerted me to two high severity issues in the checkout feature which I prioritised resolving before moving onto working on my colour prototype.

The first was that participants needed more reassurance that their payment was processed successfully; the second was that I had designed interaction with the payment fields in a way that was intuitive to me but that was not intuitive to three of my five participants. The latter was a quick fix but an important lesson in the value of testing at an early stage.

Designing the Create a Hunt Feature: a Closer Look

Each feature of my prototype went through several iterations before I arrived at my current design. Designing the Create a Hunt feature was central to my design process in terms of developing Wee Quests' personality and tone of voice. It also presented a key challenge: achieving a balance between ensuring guidance was readily available while at the same time allowing users freedom to explore...

Refining the Design

Arriving at the Prototype

As I made updates to the interface, this helped inform my writing of a style guide and then, in turn, a design language for Wee Quests. These formed the basis for translating all screens from the core features into a high fidelity colour prototype.
